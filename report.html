DRC payment-aggregator MVP — feasibility on rented rails

Date: 2026-06-04 · Built from drc-mvp-research/02-findings/ + 03-reports/ · All claims sourced inline.

Bottom line

The MVP is technically feasible on rented rails today. A merchant-facing app — merchants accept payments from customers across DRC's three biggest networks (Vodacom M-Pesa, Airtel Money, Orange Money — ~99% of DRC mobile money subscribers) — can be built using one aggregator integration. The two-leg flow (collect from the customer → bridge → settle to the merchant) is supported end-to-end with publicly verifiable pricing on pawaPay. The merchant absorbs the fee (MDR); the customer pays the exact sticker price. Round-trip cost envelope is 3.5%–5.0% before FX — so the 1% MDR placeholder is below cost, and real pricing is an open decision. Sandbox is open and self-serve.

The main constraints are:

  • Unit economics on low-value tickets. Sub-$10 round-trips are uneconomic at 4–5%.
  • Afrimoney coverage gap. <1% market share; hard zero on pawaPay; only MOKO Afrika among the four researched aggregators explicitly covers Afrimoney.
  • Legal/licensing flag. DRC's BCC framework appears to require foreign PSPs to partner with a licensed local entity — a separate track per 00-overview/research-goals.md.

Recommendation

Build the MVP on pawaPay as the single aggregator. Address payees by MSISDN (+243…) across Vodacom M-Pesa, Airtel, and Orange. Defer Afrimoney to v2 (MOKO Afrika is the fallback if Afrimoney becomes material). Don't integrate operators directly for the MVP — that's four contracts and four opaque pricing conversations to replicate something pawaPay already publishes. Open a separate legal/licensing track immediately.

Other cost components

  • FX (CDF ↔ USD): 2–5% above interbank as a DRC PSP market norm — PayAtlas. Secondary
  • Settlement to bank: 1,000–3,000 CDF (~$0.35–$1.10) per payout per DRC norm; cadence 2–5 business days; delays >$10k USD under BCC review.

Ticket-size implication: Round-trip cost is ~3.5%–5.0% before FX. Acceptable for $20+ tickets; tight for $10–$20; punishing under $10. Plan v1 economics around mid-ticket merchant payments.

Direct operator API per-leg fees

None of the four operators publishes API-driven merchant fees for DRC. M-Pesa's onboarding doc explicitly defers: "the M-Pesa Business team will review the application and inform you of the M-Pesa charging model." For modelling, pawaPay's "MMO portion" (1.5%–2%) is the best public proxy for what operators charge an aggregator.

Payee identification

The payee is a registered merchant, identified by a till / short-code the customer dials or scans (a QR carrying a tel: USSD dial-through). Settlement lands on the merchant's mobile-money number (captured at onboarding). Merchant onboarding is in scope (settlement account + till; merchant KYC flagged); MSISDN remains the underlying addressing for the settlement leg.

KYC & onboarding

Payer KYC rides existing SIM/operator wallet KYC. Aggregators run merchant KYC on the integrating business, not end-users. Licensing posture is the key flag — DRC may require foreign PSPs to partner with a licensed local entity.

Regulatory framework

  • Banque Centrale du Congo (BCC) supervises banks, EMIs, PSPs, and FX.
  • Directive #24 (Dec 2011) — original e-money framework. findevgateway case study. Secondary
  • BCC Instruction No. 15 (2018) — current KYC, CDD, record-keeping rules. voveid. Secondary
  • Law No. 04/016 (2004) — base AML/CFT law. Conflict noted: some sources cite Law No. 18/004 (2018) as the operative AML law — verify against BCC primary.
  • CENAREF / CENTIF — DRC FIU. Sources disagree on the acronym — flag for verification.
  • Data protection: Law No. 18/023 (2018); CNPDCP oversight.

Strategic option: build our own aggregator?

The software is the easiest part — the hard parts are commercial, regulatory, and operational. Building our own DRC aggregator is technically feasible (GSMA spec is open; ~$100–180k initial; 4–6 months by 3–6 senior engineers), but it eliminates only pawaPay's ~1% per-leg margin (~2% round-trip), not the operator fees (which we'd inherit directly and likely pay more for as a small player). The formal gate is the BCC EMI licence — US$2.5M paid-up capital, special-purpose subsidiary, 6–18 month application. The unseen gate is four bilateral commercial relationships with operators that don't publish per-leg API fees. Recommendation: rent now, own later — pawaPay for v1, hybrid model from v3+.

Layer 1 — Technical (the easiest part)

  • Engineering benchmark (IntaSend cost analysis Secondary): 3–6 senior developers for 4–6 months; 1–2 security specialists; $100k–$180k initial to first transaction; 30–40% of ongoing engineering on payment infrastructure maintenance.
  • Spec exists. The GSMA Mobile Money API Specification v1.2 covers all 8 use cases (merchant payments, disbursements, P2P, etc.) with open-source SDKs in Java, JavaScript, Node.js, PHP, and Android.
  • DRC adopters: Afrimoney announced GSMA-spec adoption Dec 2023. M-Pesa, Airtel, and Orange use proprietary-but-similar REST APIs. A single internal abstraction layer over four operator APIs is achievable.
  • Real-world friction at scale: "One Tanzania-focused fintech discovered their M-Pesa integration failed at higher volumes due to undocumented rate limits" (IntaSend). Webhook reliability needs redundant systems. Reconciliation discrepancies "run into the hundreds daily" in high-volume environments.

Verdict on this layer: moderately hard, well-understood, ~$100–180k + a year of senior engineering. Not the gate.

Layer 2 — Commercial (the actual moat)

Without an aggregator's cover, we onboard with each of the four operators directly:

Vodacom M-Pesa DRCEmail supportmpesa@m-pesa.cd → KYC pack → sign M-Pesa Services Agreement → 6/7-digit merchant code.

Pricing reality: None of the four operators publishes API per-leg fees for DRC. M-Pesa's onboarding page states it explicitly: "the M-Pesa Business team will review the application and inform you of the M-Pesa charging model." We can't model unit economics in advance, and as a small player we likely get quoted at or above pawaPay's pass-through rates — not below.

The very existence of pawaPay and Onafriq is because direct operator integration was so painful for ordinary merchants. They aggregated the pain. The DRC operators are not at Kenya/Nigeria openness levels — DRC sits behind operators like Kenya's Daraja in API maturity.

Verdict on this layer: this is the actual moat. Four bilateral commercial relationships, opaque pricing, no startup-favourable negotiating position. Months to years of relationship building.

Layer 4 — Float / Treasury / Operations

  • Float = sum of prefunds in operator-side accounts + in-flight settlements − customer-payable balances. To send a payout, our pawaPay-equivalent account at Vodacom must already be funded.
  • Liquidity management cost: per IFC's Liquidity Management for Mobile Money Providers toolkit — "consumes 20–30% of total expenses" for mobile money retail agents (search summary; PDF body not extracted; Medium confidence — planning anchor).
  • FX: DRC mobile money runs in CDF and USD. PayAtlas cites DRC PSP norm of 2–5% FX markup above interbank. Without our own interbank book we pay this to an FX provider.
  • Operations: 24/7 monitoring, reconciliation timing-gap exceptions running "into the hundreds daily" at scale, recertification when operator APIs change.

Verdict on this layer: materially more expensive than the software build. The day-zero requirement includes millions of dollars in operator prefunds + working capital + an FX line + an ops team.

When does building pay off? A cost model

Assumptions (illustrative — order-of-magnitude, not for budgeting):

Recommended path: rent now, hybrid later

  1. v1 (now): Rent pawaPay. Ship consumer UX. Build volume. Learn what fails in production.
  2. v2 (12–18 mo): Add MOKO Afrika as Afrimoney fallback if needed. Open early commercial conversations with M-Pesa DRC + Airtel DRC directly — sandbox in their portals — to start the relationship clock.
  3. v3 (24–36 mo, volume-permitting): Stand up our own technical layer over direct M-Pesa + Airtel contracts (the two largest DRC operators, ~82% of mobile money share combined). Keep pawaPay as fall-through for Orange + Afrimoney until volume justifies separate contracts.
  4. v4 (only if scale justifies): Apply for BCC EMI licence, commit the $2.5M capital, fully own the rails.

MVP user flow (pawaPay path)

  1. Customer initiates on their own phone. Scans the merchant's QR (a tel: USSD dial-through) or dials the till *123*1001# — no app, no internet — then enters the amount and confirms. The backend resolves the merchant from the till. (Fallback: the merchant charges the customer by number.)
  2. Backend calls pawaPay's deposit (collection) API for the customer's number on their operator. pawaPay triggers a USSD/STK PIN prompt on the customer's phone in their operator's own UX (M-Pesa / Airtel Money / Orange Money PIN). The customer pays the exact sticker price.
  3. Funds land in the pawaPay clearing balance mid-transfer (held at pawaPay, not by us).
  4. Backend calls pawaPay's payout (settlement) API for amount − fee to the merchant's number — the merchant absorbs the MDR. The merchant sees the payment in their app/dashboard.
  5. Settlement to bank (if the merchant cashes out beyond mobile money) follows pawaPay's cadence — DRC PSP market norm is 2–5 business days.

Sample economics — $20 round-trip, M-Pesa payer → Orange merchant

  • Collection (M-Pesa, 2.5%): $0.50
  • Disbursement (Orange, 1.0%): $0.20
  • Total before FX: $0.70 (3.5%)
  • + FX 2–5% if currency switch: $0.40–$1.00
  • + Settlement fee per payout (DRC norm): $0.35–$1.10
