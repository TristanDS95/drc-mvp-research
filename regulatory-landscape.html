Regulatory landscape & the revised path

Created 2026-07-01 · Revised the same day after the product owner challenged the switch conclusions: the delivery-record evidence was added and every switch-dependent claim recalibrated from fact to conditional. · A companion to the path to profitability. That note asked "which fee structure do we move to, and what does that structure require of us?" This note answers the "what does it require" half from primary sources - the actual central-bank instructions - and revises the path accordingly.
The short answer, in three sentences. The cheapest legal path to cutting our fees is real and light: register a Congolese company and obtain the central bank's "payment aggregator" authorization (a paperwork dossier, ~60-day decision clock, no stated capital minimum - the same permit pawaPay itself runs on), then integrate directly with the mobile networks to cut out pawaPay's ~2% markup. Forget the $2.5M e-money licence and the float-holding idea - only the four MNOs' own subsidiaries have ever obtained that licence, and holding money buys nothing the light path doesn't. The government's "interoperability switch" is mostly noise - announced twice since 2020, still no proof it works for mobile money - and the fact that five years of cross-network transfers never gave merchants a way to accept payments is exactly why this app has a business.
What this document is - and isn't. Like its companion, this is a strategy and logic note. Claims traced to a recorded finding are tagged research (here, most trace to two new findings files built from BCC primary documents: national-switch-interoperability.md and licensing-categories.md). Product-owner calls are tagged stance. Unknowns are tagged open. Nothing here is legal advice - the explicit next step is DRC counsel.

How to read this

The path-to-profitability note reasoned from the world as the June-2026 research corpus described it: a fragmented market where interoperability is scarce (that scarcity being our product), and where the only way to hold and net funds is to become a $2.5M-capitalised Electronic Money Institution. Both premises were true when the underlying sources were written. Neither is quite true now. This note walks what changed, then re-derives the path with the fewest and most minor regulatory hoops.

What changed, part 1: interoperability became the law

BCC Instruction n°58 (signed 4 Sep 2024, in force 4 Mar 2025) is the document that changes the game. It was read in full for this note. What it says: research (national-switch-interoperability.md, rg-001)

  • Every electronic payment instrument must be accepted by every covered institution (art. 4) - and the covered institutions explicitly include banks, e-money issuers (the MNO wallets), monétique operators, and "les agrégateurs" (art. 2).
  • Everyone must connect to the Switch Monétique National (art. 6). Not "may" - must.
  • Transactions routed by the switch are cleared on a multilateral net basis and settled at the central bank (arts. 11-12). Participants without a central-bank settlement account participate indirectly through one that has it.
  • Fees are negotiated between the switch manager and participants (arts. 14-15) - nothing is published yet. Art. 16 sets who pays: on merchant transactions, the acquirer pays and charges the merchant; on transfers between institutions, the sender's institution pays.
Read that middle bullet again. Multilateral net clearing of cross-network flows is exactly what the treasury/float model in Avenue 2 was designed to do privately - hold a balance per network, cancel offsetting flows, move only the net. The regulator is building that as shared national infrastructure, for all participants, with settlement at the central bank. If it delivers, the netting benefit no longer requires us to hold anyone's money. And note what the mandate does not say: nowhere does it make interop free. Arts. 14-16 institutionalise fees at every layer, and the merchant-commission rule presupposes acquirers who charge merchants - the switch interconnects institutions; it does not serve merchants. That acquirer seat is our business, with or without the switch.

What changed, part 2: Mosolo and GMIC - announcements vs delivery

What the announcements say: research (news sources rg-004 to rg-008; see the finding for caveats)

  • The Prime Minister announced the launch of the switch, named "Mosolo", on 2 May 2025 - with press coverage explicitly promising that an M-Pesa user can transfer to an Orange Money subscriber and vice versa, for roughly 30 million mobile money users.
  • The interbank body that will run it - the GMIC (Groupement Monétique Interbancaire du Congo), a co-owned economic-interest group with IFC (World Bank Group) backing - was reported in January 2026 as launching by end-March 2026, covering instant transfers across banks, microfinance institutions, and mobile money.
  • Card-side integrations exist (Multipay connected to the switch for Mosolo card transactions, Dec 2024) - the switch is real infrastructure for cards.
The delivery record says: discount heavily. Three facts, added after this note's conclusions were challenged, argue that DRC interop announcements systematically outrun delivery: research (national-switch-interoperability.md, "The delivery record")
  • The switch has been "launched" before. The Switch Monétique National first launched on 23 December 2020; July-2021 press already credited its "operationalization" - nominally covering mobile money - to the outgoing BCC governor as a legacy achievement. Four years later the same switch was re-announced as "Mosolo."
  • The mandate is not new either. Instruction 42 already required all public payment platforms to be interoperable in March 2020. The corpus's June-2026 fieldwork found the market still fragmented six years on. Instruction 58 is the regulator's second attempt - evidence the first did not self-execute.
  • Live P2P interop never closed our gap. Orange's "Kabola na voisin" has let Orange Money customers send directly to M-Pesa users since ~2021, at ~1% standard rates (with KYC-upgrade friction). Five years of working wallet-to-wallet transfers produced no cross-network merchant acceptance market - the gap this app targets.
And as of this writing: no evidence found of GMIC's actual creation, of switch transaction volumes, or of any consumer-facing documentation of switch-routed wallet transfers. open
The other load-bearing unknown is the switch's transaction pricing: Instruction 58 leaves it to negotiation and nothing is published. A regional analogy (GIM-UEMOA caps interoperable withdrawals at a flat ~500 FCFA) suggests switches price flat and low - but that is an analogy, not a DRC fact. open

Why this matters to us: a conditional threat, a conditional gift, and one solid fact

The threat - conditional, and weaker than it first looks. If GMIC materialises and wallet-to-wallet switch routing becomes real and frictionless, "we bridge the networks" erodes as a differentiator. But the delivery record cuts both ways: P2P transfer interop has existed for five years without producing merchant acceptance, because transfers and acceptance are different products - a merchant needs a till that works across networks, instant confirmation, records, reconciliation, and refunds, none of which a switch provides. Instruction 58's own fee rules presuppose acquirers who serve and charge merchants; the switch interconnects institutions and leaves the merchant-facing seat open. The strategy conclusion stands, but for a stronger reason than "the switch is coming": the moat is merchant tooling, not routing - and it always was. Watch the leading indicators (GMIC's OHADA registration, published tariffs, operator help pages documenting switch-routed transfers) rather than the press releases. stance / open

The gift - also conditional. Our 3.5-5% cost floor exists because a cross-network payment today is two full aggregator legs (fees-and-costs.md). If mobile money flows ever route through the national switch, the cross-network leg should cost an MNO fee plus a switch commission instead - and as an explicitly covered institution, an authorized aggregator is both obliged and entitled to connect. The structure of a lower cost floor is being built; whether it functions, and at what price, is unproven. Do not build the cost model on it. open

The solid fact underneath both: whichever way the switch goes, the fewest-hoops path (next section) does not depend on it. The aggregator authorization and direct MNO deals stand on their own; the switch is upside if it works and irrelevant if it stalls. stance

What changed, part 3: the licence is a ladder, not a wall

The path-to-profitability note treated regulation as binary: pass-through (no licence) or custody (EMI, $2.5M, 6-18 months). Reading the primary texts shows three rungs, and the corpus only knew about the top one. research (licensing-categories.md)

Rung 1 - Payment aggregator authorization

Light · the one we need
An autorisation as a "connected services provider" under BCC Instruction n°42 (2020), art. 9.
  • What it takes: a locally incorporated company (OHADA SARL) and a document dossier - statutes, shareholders and directors (with good-character evidence), certified or projected financials, business plan, technical certificates, standard SLA, technical specs, settlement and risk procedures. No minimum capital appears in the connected-services provisions. The law behind it (Loi 18/019, arts. 108-110) gives the BCC a 60-day decision clock from a complete dossier.
  • Who has it: the BCC publishes the registry. pawaPay operates in the DRC as "Kerry Payments SARLU" on this exact authorization - not an EMI licence - alongside MFS Africa, Maxicash, FreshPay, CinetPay, Multipay and dozens more. Paymetrust got the same approval in July 2025, so the route is being actively granted.
  • What it proves: this authorization is sufficient to run multi-MNO collect/payout rails commercially in the DRC. It is the gateway to direct MNO deals, and it is the category Instruction 58 names for switch connection.
research Instruction n°42 arts. 1, 7, 9 (read page-by-page); BCC aggregator registry; Loi 18/019 arts. 108-110; Paymetrust approval coverage. All in licensing-categories.md.

Rung 2 - Payment institution (établissement de paiement)

Middle · the bridge, if needed
An agrément as a société financière under the 2022 banking law (Loi 22/069, art. 168) and BCC Instruction n°53.
  • What it is: the 2022 law created "payment institutions" as a category of financial company, with EMIs as a subset - a European-style ladder the corpus never saw. This is the plausible home for holding funds in transit (acquiring-style custody, merchant settlement) without issuing stored value.
  • What it takes: a locally incorporated Congolese company, a notarized dossier, paid-up capital deposited at a local bank "at least equal to the minimum fixed by the BCC," and a 90-day decision clock.
  • The missing number: the instruction fixing the capital minimum for non-EMI payment institutions was not found (bank capital is $50M, microfinance $50k-$300k, EMI $2.5M; nothing located in between). If this figure is modest, the treasury decision stops being a $2.5M decision. This is the #1 question for counsel. open
research Instruction n°53 arts. 1-11 (read page-by-page); Loi 22/069 category structure. See licensing-categories.md.

Rung 3 - EMI licence

Heavy · probably never
The custody licence the path-to-profitability note treated as Avenue 2's price of admission.
  • The $2.5M paid-up capital figure is confirmed by a second independent source, upgrading the corpus's confidence in it.
  • The sobering detail: the only four licensed EMIs in the DRC are Vodacash, Airtel Money RDC, Orange Money RDC, and Afrimobile Money - all MNO subsidiaries. There is no independent-startup-EMI precedent. The route is heavier in practice than on paper.
  • And its payoff is squeezed from both sides: if the national switch delivers, the licence's main prize (private netting of cross-network flows) is provided centrally without custody; if the switch stalls, the model's original obstacles stand unchanged - MNO hostility to fee circumvention, treasury operations, and a licence no independent player has ever obtained. Neither branch favours it. What remains unique to the EMI is float income and FX spread - real, but a different, later business.
research rg-012 corroborating mp-010/xo-005; see licensing-categories.md.
An uncomfortable implication for today, not just tomorrow. The current design rests on the inference that a thin pass-through wrapper inherits pawaPay's regulatory posture. But the BCC authorizes aggregators and even "e-commerce platform managers" by name, Instruction 42 requires operators to verify their local partners hold BCC authorization, and our merchant-facing app plausibly fits the aggregator definition itself. The corpus's #1 legal flag was right to worry - but the answer it feared (EMI-or-partner) turns out to be a small hoop instead: the authorization we may already need is the light one, and it's the same one every path forward needs anyway. Whether the beta needs it before commercial launch is a counsel question. open

What happens to the two avenues

The path-to-profitability note argued the two avenues were one staged path, with the treasury model (Avenue 2) as the leverage that makes direct MNO deals (Avenue 1) possible. The primary sources flip that logic twice over. stance

  • On hoops, the order was backwards. Avenue 1 needs only the Rung-1 authorization pawaPay itself holds. Avenue 2 (as originally conceived) needs the heaviest licence in the market, one no independent player has ever obtained.
  • On incentives, the leverage story was shaky. Private float-netting removes cross-network transaction revenue from the MNOs. From their side it looks like fee circumvention - and the float-netting brainstorm itself noted that MNO anti-money-laundering systems flag and freeze wallets behaving like processors. A player netting at scale without a licence is not a partner they court; it's an account they freeze. This holds with or without a functioning switch; if the switch does deliver, tolerance for a private workaround only drops further.
  • The on-net tension mostly dissolves. The companion note framed free on-net facilitation as a future "leak" from the treasury model. If the treasury model is off the table (on either branch above), free on-net facilitation stays what it is today - a cheap merchant-acquisition feature that never touches custody - and can stay free permanently.
  • The supplier-risk counterweight is direct MNO deals, not a private treasury. The strongest argument for Avenue 2 was that pawaPay sets our cost floor, sees our volume, and could compete with us. That risk is real and remains. The dependable counterweight is Stage 1 below (own MNO relationships on our own authorization); the public switch is a second counterweight only if it materialises.

The staged plan, updated

  1. MVP (unchanged): prove adoption on rented rails, unprofitable on purpose. Everything the beta was going to measure - adoption, the on-net/cross-net mix, willingness to pay - it should still measure. stance
  2. Stage 1 - become a real DRC player (light hoops): incorporate the DRC subsidiary, obtain the aggregator authorization (60-day clock), and open direct merchant/disbursement integrations with M-Pesa and Airtel (82% of the market) with pawaPay as fallback. This kills pawaPay's ~2% round-trip margin - the only part of the fee stack the old Avenue 1 could ever remove. research (fee decomposition per own-aggregator.md)
  3. Stage 2 - ride the switch, if and when it materialises: connect to Mosolo/GMIC (nominally mandatory for an authorized aggregator anyway), settling indirectly through a partner bank. If the mobile-money leg actually functions, the cross-network leg drops from two full legs to an MNO fee plus a switch commission, with netting done centrally. Gate this stage on the leading indicators (GMIC's legal creation, published tariffs, documented switch-routed transfers) - not on announcements - and do not build the cost model on it. open
  4. Stage 3 - custody, only if the data demands it: if float income / FX spread / instant settlement become the business, take the payment-institution agrément (if counsel confirms its capital is modest) - not the EMI licence, unless issuing stored value ever becomes the product. This remains the "different company" decision the companion note described, but with a potentially much lower price tag. open

What to do next, in order

  1. Verify GMIC/Mosolo status and pricing. Did GMIC launch on its end-March-2026 target? What are the participation categories and fees for an aggregator? This is now the single highest-value research question, ahead of MNO rate quotes. The BCC's payment-systems surveillance division is the named contact point. open
  2. Engage DRC counsel with three specific questions, not a general review: (a) does the current merchant-facing app on pawaPay rails already require the aggregator authorization; (b) what minimum capital has the BCC fixed for non-EMI payment institutions; (c) what are GMIC participation requirements for a non-bank. open
  3. Plan the DRC subsidiary now. Every path, including the current one, points at a locally incorporated company. It is a knowable, bounded cost, and it starts every clock. stance
  4. Keep the beta's measurement goals exactly as written. Adoption, the on-net/cross-net mix, and a real willingness-to-pay signal survive all of this unchanged - and willingness to pay remains the biggest unknown under the whole plan. open
  5. Update the strategy's language: the moat is merchant tooling, not routing; the two avenues become the three stages above; and the EMI moves from "the bigger lever" to "probably never." stance

  • Instruction n°58's text (mandate, covered institutions incl. aggregators, multilateral clearing, fee incidence) - High. Primary document, read in full. research
  • Aggregator authorization regime (dossier, 60-day clock, no stated capital; pawaPay = Kerry Payments SARLU) - High. Primary instruction + regulator registry. research
  • Payment-institution tier exists; local incorporation required; 90-day clock - High. Primary instruction. Its minimum capital - not found; the #1 counsel question. open
  • EMI $2.5M - Medium-High (two independent sources; BCC's own Instruction 24 text still not directly read). research
  • The delivery record (switch first launched Dec 2020; interop mandated since 2020; operator P2P interop live since ~2021 without producing merchant acceptance) - High. Press and operator primary pages. This is the basis for treating switch-dependent conclusions as conditionals. research
  • Mosolo announcement (May 2025); GMIC targeted end-March 2026 - Medium that the announcements happened (consistent multi-outlet news); Low that the mobile-money leg functions; no evidence found of GMIC's creation, switch volumes, or consumer-facing switch-routed transfers. open
  • Switch transaction pricing - None. Negotiated per Instruction 58, nothing public. The claim that switch routing would beat the 3.5-5% two-leg cost is a structural inference about an unproven system, not a verified number. open
  • Merchant willingness to pay - None. Unchanged from the companion note; still the hinge the whole plan turns on. open
