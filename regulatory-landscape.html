Regulatory landscape & the revised path

The short answer, in three sentences. The cheapest legal path to cutting our fees is real and light: register a Congolese company and obtain the central bank's "payment aggregator" permit (a paperwork dossier, ~60-day decision clock, no stated capital minimum - the same permit pawaPay itself runs on), then integrate directly with the mobile networks to cut out pawaPay's ~2% markup. Forget the $2.5M e-money licence and the money-holding idea - the central bank's own register shows only the four phone networks' subsidiaries have ever held that licence, and holding money buys nothing the light path doesn't. The government's "interoperability switch" is mostly noise - announced twice since 2020, still no proof it works for mobile money - and the fact that five years of cross-network transfers never gave merchants a way to accept payments is exactly why this app has a business.

The three permits, plainly

DRC law offers three levels of permission for payment companies. The old plan assumed only the heaviest existed. Reading the central bank's (BCC's) own rules shows a ladder: research (licensing-categories.md)

1 · Aggregator permit - the one we need

Light
Official name: "autorisation" as a payment-systems aggregator (BCC Instruction 42).
  • What it takes: a locally registered Congolese company plus a paperwork dossier (company statutes, who owns and runs it, financial projections, business plan, technical specs, risk procedures). No minimum capital is stated, and the law gives the BCC 60 days to decide once the dossier is complete.
  • Proof it works: the BCC publishes a register of approved aggregators - pawaPay is on it (as "Kerry Payments"), alongside CinetPay, MFS Africa, Maxicash and dozens more. This permit is sufficient to run multi-network payment rails commercially in the DRC.
  • One caveat: the BCC's published register looks stale - Paymetrust's July 2025 approval (reported by two independent outlets) isn't on it yet. Treat the register as indicative, not real-time.

2 · Payment institution - the maybe-later bridge

Middle
Official name: "établissement de paiement," a category created by the 2022 banking law.
  • This is the plausible home for briefly holding money in transit (collecting from customers and settling to merchants ourselves) without becoming a full e-money issuer.
  • What it takes: a Congolese company, a notarized dossier, a 90-day decision clock, and paid-up capital "as fixed by the BCC" - the amount is not published anywhere we could find. If it is modest, holding funds stops being a $2.5M decision. This is the #1 question for DRC counsel. open

3 · E-money licence - probably never

Heavy
Official name: établissement de monnaie électronique (EMI), the licence for issuing stored value and holding float.
  • ~$2.5M of paid-up capital, a 6-18 month process - and the BCC's own register (fetched directly, 2026-07-01) lists exactly four holders ever: Vodacash (M-Pesa), Airtel Money RDC, Orange Money RDC, and Afrimobile Money. All are phone-network subsidiaries; no independent company has one. research
  • Its main prize for us (privately netting cross-network flows) is either delivered centrally by the national switch (if that ever works) or blocked by the networks' hostility to fee circumvention (if it doesn't). Neither branch justifies the licence. stance

Do we need a permit already?

Possibly. The current app - a merchant-facing service aggregating payments across networks on pawaPay's rails - plausibly fits the BCC's own definition of an "aggregator," and the rules require local payment partners to hold BCC authorization. The good news: the permit in question is the light one, and every future step needs it anyway. Whether the beta needs it before commercial launch is a question for counsel, not a reason to slow down. open

The national switch: the law vs what actually exists

On paper, since March 2025 every payment player in the DRC - including aggregators - must connect to a state-run national switch ("Mosolo") that makes every network's money usable everywhere, with the central bank clearing the net flows. We read the instruction in full; the mandate is real. research (national-switch-interoperability.md)

In practice, the delivery record says not to build plans on it:

  • The switch was first launched in December 2020, credited to the then-governor as a legacy achievement - then re-announced as "Mosolo" in May 2025 as if new.
  • Interoperability was already mandated in 2020; the market stayed fragmented anyway. The 2024 instruction is the regulator's second attempt.
  • Operator-to-operator transfers (Orange to M-Pesa, "Kabola na voisin") have existed since ~2021 at 1% - and five years of that never produced cross-network merchant acceptance. Transfers and acceptance are different products.
  • As of 2026-07-01 (re-checked by a dedicated research pass): no evidence the switch's governing body (GMIC) was ever legally created, no published prices, no proof mobile-money payments flow through it. Its card side is real; its mobile-money side is a promise.
What the mandate does and doesn't mean for us. It does not make interop free - the instruction itself sets up fees at every layer and assumes acquirers who serve and charge merchants. That acquirer seat is our business, with or without the switch. If the switch ever works, it becomes a cheaper rail we are entitled to plug into (upside); if it never does, nothing in our plan changes. Watch it with hard indicators - GMIC legally registered, real prices published, operator pages documenting switch-routed transfers - and ignore launch announcements. stance

What to do next, in order

  1. Engage DRC counsel with three narrow questions: (a) does the current app already need the aggregator permit; (b) what capital does the payment-institution permit require; (c) what are the switch's participation terms for a non-bank, if any exist yet. open
  2. Plan the Congolese subsidiary now. Every path requires a locally registered company; it is a bounded cost and it starts every clock. stance
  3. Keep the beta's measurements exactly as planned - adoption, the same-network mix, and what merchants will pay. Nothing here changes them. stance
