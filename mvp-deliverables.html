MVP deliverables & goals — to code freeze

Date: 2026-06-04 · 16 weeks · Endpoint: app fully built & tested, ready to go live. For per-person task detail see the detailed schedule.

Overview

Over 16 weeks, the app goes from nothing to a complete, tested merchant-acceptance product running on the payment partner's test system — built, proven, and frozen, ready to switch to live whenever the business gives the go-ahead. It lets a merchant accept mobile-money payments from a customer on any network; the customer pays the exact sticker price (no app needed — they pay by USSD / QR), and the merchant absorbs our fee.

The first four weeks (Foundation) stand up the team, tools, designs, and accounts, resolve the central legal question, and prove a single test payment can flow through the payment partner. The next twelve weeks (Build) turn that proven plumbing into the full product: the money-records backbone, merchant onboarding (settlement account + till), the two halves of a payment (collecting from the customer and settling to the merchant), the customer's no-app USSD / QR way to pay, the headline cross-network capability, history and receipts, an automatic safety net, internal support tools, thorough failure-handling, full French, performance on low-cost phones, and a final hardening pass into code freeze.

Each week is broken into its Goal(s), the concrete Tasks, and the open questions to Research (investigations to resolve that week). External dependencies that could delay us — legal sign-off, accounts, partner approvals — are tracked separately under Gates & dependencies so the build goals read cleanly. Code freeze means we stop adding new features and only fix bugs — the app is fully built and proven on the test ("sandbox") environment and ready to flip to the real, live system. Going live (publishing to the app stores, real money, real users) is the next phase.

Primary goals — what these 16 weeks deliver

Everything in the schedule ladders up to six concrete outcomes:

1

A working merchant-acceptance app

A polished merchant app (iPhone + Android) where a merchant signs up, sets where they get paid, and accepts a customer's payment in a few taps. The customer needs no app — they pay by USSD / QR from any phone.

2

Cross-network acceptance proven

The core promise: a merchant on one mobile money network (e.g. Vodacom) can accept a customer paying from any other (e.g. Airtel or Orange) — something merchants can't easily do today.

3

Trustworthy & safe

The customer always pays the exact sticker price (the merchant absorbs our fee), automatic refunds to the customer when a payment fails, and a precise internal record of every transaction.

4

Built for the real DRC

French throughout, works on low-cost Android phones and shaky networks, and meets customers where they are — paying by USSD from a basic phone, sharing proof on WhatsApp.

5

The legal path clarified

A clear answer from counsel on whether we can operate on our payment partner's license or need our own — the single biggest unknown, resolved early.

6

Ready to launch on demand

Fully tested, documented, and frozen — so the business can flip the switch to "live" whenever conditions are right, with no scramble.

Gates & dependencies

These are things outside the building work that we depend on. Most are quick and low-risk; one (legal) is the real watch item. They're listed here so they're visible and owned, without cluttering the weekly build goals. A diamond (◆) marks weeks that involve a gate.

Legal opinion — do we need our own financial license?
What it is: A written answer from DRC fintech counsel on whether our pass-through model (we never hold the money) lets us operate under the payment partner's existing license, or whether we need our own license from the central bank.
Starts: Week 1  ·  Needed by: Week 4 (it shapes the architecture)  ·  Risk if it slips: High — the biggest single unknown in the plan. If counsel says we need our own license, that's a much larger, more expensive path. Front-loaded to Week 1 precisely so we learn the answer early.
Payment partner — test access (instant)
What it is: A free, self-serve account on the partner's test environment (fake-money sandbox). No approval needed.
Starts: Week 2  ·  Risk: Low — sign up and go.
Payment partner — live-money approval (for going live, not this build)
What it is: The partner's verification of our business before we can move real money. We start it early so the clock is running, but it only blocks going live (the next phase) — not this build, which uses the test environment.
Starts: Week 2 (early)  ·  Needed by: next phase  ·  Risk to this 16-week plan: Low — runs in the background, gates no build deliverable here.
App store accounts
What it is: Apple Developer account ($99/yr) + Google Play Console account ($25 one-time) — required to publish later.
Starts: Week 2  ·  Risk: Low — 1–2 days each.
Test SIM cards & phones
What it is: Real DRC SIM cards (Vodacom, Airtel, Orange) and 1–2 low-cost Android phones (the kind most Congolese use) for realistic testing.
Starts: Week 3  ·  Needed by: Week 4 (sign-up testing)  ·  Risk: Low — simple procurement, ~$250–$450 total.

Phase 0 — Foundation (Weeks 1-4)

Get the team, tools, designs, and external clocks all in motion — and prove a single test payment can flow through the payment partner before the real building starts.

Week 1
Kickoff & legal clock started
Foundation
Goal
The team, tools, and the most important external clock (legal) are all in motion, and an empty app builds on both phone types.
Tasks
  • Stand up the code repository and automatic checks — set up where the code lives plus continuous integration (every change is automatically tested before it's accepted).
  • Create the empty app — a blank app that compiles for iPhone and Android from one shared codebase, plus a basic cloud server that answers a health check.
  • Engage DRC fintech counsel with the licensing question (see Gates).
  • Set up project tools — task tracking, team chat, shared documents.
  • Competitor teardown — study how Wave, M-Pesa, and Orange Money apps look and behave.
Research — open questions
  • Which legal entity must own the eventual app-store listings? (Regulators sometimes require the licensed financial provider to be the publisher.)
  • Which cloud region sits closest to DRC for the lowest delay to users?
Week 2
Architecture locked & accounts opened
Foundation
Goal
We know exactly how the system is structured, every account is open, and the test environment is reachable.
Tasks
  • Write the architecture blueprint — how money flows, how data is stored, and how our server talks to the payment partner. (Like a building's structural drawings before construction.)
  • Design the payment "state machine" — the fixed set of stages every payment passes through, so its status is never ambiguous.
  • Open the test environment — the partner's fake-money sandbox.
  • Open app-store accounts — Apple and Google.
  • First rough screen sketches (wireframes).
Research — open questions
  • Exactly which capabilities does the partner's interface offer — collecting money, paying out to a merchant, status checks? Read the documentation in depth and confirm.
  • Which database design best fits a money ledger that must always balance?
Week 3
Designs done & app shell navigable
Foundation
Goal
The full look of the app exists in design, and you can tap through the empty screens.
Tasks
  • Finish the visual designs — all screens and pop-ups, in French, in the coral brand style (detailed mockups, not yet working software).
  • Build the reusable component kit — the shared design system (buttons, inputs, cards) every screen is assembled from.
  • Wire up navigation so you can move between the empty screens.
  • Set up the translation system — a framework that swaps every piece of text by language, even though we start French-only.
Research — open questions
  • Does the coral palette render well on low-cost Android screens, which often show colors differently?
  • Does the chosen font display French accents cleanly at small sizes?
Week 4
Merchant sign-up works & first test payment
Foundation
Goal
A real DRC merchant can sign up and set an app passcode, and the system can push a first payment through the partner's test environment.
Tasks
  • Merchant phone sign-up — enter a number, receive a one-time code by text message, you're in. Under a minute. (Fuller business verification — KYC — is flagged for later.)
  • App passcode setup — the merchant sets a passcode that unlocks the merchant app. (The customer never uses our app — they approve on their own operator's prompt.)
  • Secure login — keep merchants signed in using session tokens (digital passes), revocable by our server.
  • Connect to the payment partner — build the connector and trigger a first test "take money" request; confirm the partner's status message (webhook) is received.
Research — open questions
  • Do one-time codes actually arrive on Vodacom and Airtel lines? Validate with real SIM cards.
  • Has the legal opinion arrived, and does it hold up against the architecture we locked?
Foundation complete — the team is set, designs are done, sign-up works, and the payment plumbing is proven.

Phase 1 — Build the app (Weeks 5-16)

Turn the proven plumbing into a complete, trustworthy, polished product — built and tested entirely on the safe test environment, ending fully ready to go live.

Week 5
Money-records backbone & merchant setup
Build
Goal
The app's internal accounting system is built and rigorously tested, and a merchant can finish onboarding — set where they get paid and get their till / QR — before any real payment touches it.
Tasks
  • Build the ledger — the precise internal record of every money movement, using double-entry bookkeeping (every movement recorded twice so the totals must always balance).
  • Build merchant onboarding — record the merchant, the settlement account (the mobile-money number they get paid into), and a till / short code; generate the merchant's QR (a USSD dial-through) to display at the counter. (Fuller business verification — KYC — is flagged for later.)
  • Implement the payment state machine — the defined stages: started → money collected from customer → money settled to merchant → done / failed / refunded.
  • Add a repeat-safeguard — idempotency keys so an accidentally-repeated request only moves money once.
  • Build the automatic-refund logic and cover everything with unit tests (automated checks for each piece in isolation).
Research — open questions
  • What are the exact rules for the rare "money taken but the customer refund itself failed" case that needs a human?
  • How does the partner represent amounts (whole units vs fractions) for each currency, so rounding is never wrong?
Week 6
Accepting a payment: collecting from the customer
Build
Goal
A merchant can request a payment; the customer pays the exact amount via USSD / QR from any phone, and the operator's approval prompt appears on the customer's phone.
Tasks
  • Build the "collect" step — ask the partner to take money from the customer's wallet; the operator's familiar secret-code prompt appears on the customer's phone.
  • Build the customer-USSD / QR path — the merchant's QR is a USSD dial-through; the customer scans it or dials the till and confirms with their own operator PIN. No customer app.
  • Build the amount / request screen — the merchant enters an amount and switches between US dollars and Congolese francs; the customer is charged the exact sticker price (the merchant absorbs our fee).
  • Handle the status messages (the partner's automatic updates) that move the payment along.
Research — open questions
  • Does the USSD dial-through behave consistently across all three operators — and what's the cleanest fallback if a customer's phone can't scan?
  • How long does the approval prompt typically take, so the merchant's waiting screen timer is realistic?
Week 7
Accepting a payment: settling to the merchant
Build
Goal
Money can be settled into the merchant's wallet (net of our fee), and any failure refunds the customer automatically.
Tasks
  • Build the "settle" step — the partner sends the money on to the merchant's mobile-money wallet, and the merchant gets their operator's normal "you received money" confirmation. This is instant pass-through — we never hold the funds.
  • Generate the receipt — proof of payment, showing the gross amount, our fee, and the net the merchant received; keepable and shareable.
  • Wire the failure path — if settlement fails, the customer is automatically refunded using the backbone from Week 5.
  • Add resilient retries — retry-with-backoff for status messages (re-attempt automatically, waiting longer each time).
Research — open questions
  • What happens if a merchant's settlement number is valid but not yet a registered wallet — does the partner create one, or reject it?
  • How quickly do customer refunds actually land across the three operators?
Week 8
Full cross-network merchant payment, end-to-end
Build
Goal
The product's core promise works — a customer on one network pays a merchant on another, as one smooth flow — and the first half of the build is stabilized.
Tasks
  • Stitch the two halves into one experience — collect from the customer, then settle to the merchant, shown as a single payment with clear progress stages, working across every customer-to-merchant network combination (e.g. customer on Vodacom → merchant on Airtel).
  • Add whole-flow automated tests — end-to-end tests that exercise the full payment.
  • Stabilize — fix whatever the first half of building and the demos exposed.
  • Run live demos with advisors and pilot merchants.
Research — open questions
  • Which network combinations are slowest, and can anything speed them up?
  • What confused people in the demos, and what's the smallest change that fixes it?
Core promise proven — a full cross-network merchant payment works end-to-end. This is the heart of the product.
Week 9
History & receipts
Build
Goal
Merchants can see their past payments, daily totals, and share proof.
Tasks
  • Build the payment history list — every payment received, grouped by day with daily totals, showing clear status (settled / pending / failed) and the net amount after our fee.
  • Build shareable receipts — a clean receipt for each payment with one-tap WhatsApp sharing (the dominant way Congolese share proof of payment).
  • Make the list feel fast with skeleton screens (grey placeholders shown while data loads).
  • Generate the shareable receipt page on the server so it previews nicely in a WhatsApp message.
Research — open questions
  • How does the receipt actually look in a WhatsApp preview on real devices?
  • How long should a shared receipt link stay viewable?
Week 10
Automatic safety net
Build
Goal
The system catches money mismatches on its own, and the team is alerted instantly when anything breaks.
Tasks
  • Build automatic reconciliation — a daily scheduled comparison of our records against the partner's, flagging any mismatch.
  • Add monitoring and alerts — structured logging plus instant alerts so the team is pinged the moment something breaks.
  • Add operator-status tracking — watch each network's success rate and show a banner if one is failing.
Research — open questions
  • What success-rate level should trigger the "this network is having problems" banner?
  • How far back can the partner's records be queried for the daily comparison?
Week 11
Support tools
Build
Goal
The team can resolve a stuck merchant (or a customer's stuck payment) quickly — without needing a developer.
Tasks
  • Build the internal admin dashboard — look up any merchant or payment, issue a refund, resolve an issue, with an action log recording every staff action.
  • Add the in-app "report a problem" button — opens WhatsApp with the payment details already filled in, making support easy for the merchant.
Research — open questions
  • Walking real (test) cases through the tool, what capability is missing?
  • What should the support workflow and response-time targets be?
Week 12
Handling the weird edge cases
Build
Goal
The unusual-but-real failures are all handled gracefully, and money is always accounted for.
Tasks
  • Handle every odd scenario — an operator reversing a payment, the network dropping mid-payment, a double-tap on "send," a payment that gets stuck. Each resolves cleanly.
  • Add offline handling — queue actions, show an offline banner, and never lose or duplicate a payment when the signal drops (common in DRC).
  • Add a remote update switch — a force-update control for the rare case a critical fix must reach everyone.
Research — open questions
  • In a focused "try to break it" session, what failure modes appear that we haven't handled?
  • What should the refund and dispute policies be?
Week 13
Everything in proper French
Build
Goal
Every word in the app is correct, natural DRC French.
Tasks
  • Route all text through the translation system and fill in French, reviewed by a DRC-French speaker so nothing reads like a bad translation. (The system is built so Lingala and Swahili can be added later.)
  • Check the fit — French is longer than English, so confirm every button and label still fits on screen.
  • Add the privacy policy and terms of service — in French, lawyer-reviewed.
Research — open questions
  • Does the tone read as warm but precise to native speakers?
  • Do number, date, and currency formats match DRC conventions?
Week 14
Speed & reliability under pressure
Build
Goal
The app stays fast and reliable even under heavy use and poor networks.
Tasks
  • Run load testing — simulate many users at once and fix the bottlenecks it reveals.
  • Make status messages resilient — a backup queue retries any dropped message, with the daily reconciliation as the final safety net.
  • Tune for low-cost phones — fast startup and a small download size on the budget Android phones most Congolese use.
Research — open questions
  • Where does the system break under load, and is there comfortable headroom above expected demand?
  • Does the app recover correctly under simulated network drops, not just ideal conditions?
Week 15
Team use & bug bash
Build
Goal
The whole team uses the app daily on real phones, surfacing and fixing the last bugs.
Tasks
  • Everyone lives in the app — distribute private test builds and use it daily ("dogfooding"), finding the rough edges planned testing misses.
  • Burn down the bug list — money-correctness first, then experience, then minor polish.
  • Prepare launch marketing assets — app-store screenshots, descriptions, and graphics for the next phase.
Research — open questions
  • Are the team's day-over-day "would I recommend this?" gut-feel scores improving?
  • Which non-blocking issues should be deferred to after launch?
Week 16
Code freeze — MVP complete
Build
Goal
The app is built, tested, documented, and ready to flip live whenever the business says go.
Tasks
  • Declare code freeze — stop adding features; only fix bugs, locking the app down for launch readiness.
  • Run a security pass — check outside code libraries for known issues, confirm secrets are stored safely, and test that backups can be restored.
  • Complete documentation — how to run, support, and onboard new team members onto the system.
  • Stakeholder demo — a full walkthrough of the finished app.
Research — open questions
  • Is the payment partner's live-money approval (started in Week 2) complete or on track for the next phase?
  • Does everything pass a final readiness review against the launch checklist?
MVP complete — the app is fully built, tested, and ready to launch. Going live (app stores, real money, real users) is the next phase.

Glossary — plain English

Every technical term used in this document, explained simply.

Payment partner / "rails"
The company whose infrastructure we rent to actually move money. Instead of us building a separate connection to each mobile money network (Vodacom, Airtel, Orange), the partner has already done that — we connect once to them, and they reach all the networks. "Rails" is the industry word for this kind of underlying payment infrastructure (like railroad tracks that trains run on).
Aggregator
A company (like our payment partner) that bundles many payment networks together behind a single connection, so businesses don't have to integrate each one separately.
Test environment ("sandbox")
A complete fake-money copy of the payment system, used for safe testing. You can run thousands of payments through it without any real money moving and without risk. The entire 16-week build happens here.
Going live ("production")
The opposite of the test environment — the real, live system with real money and real customers. Reaching it is the next phase, after this 16-week plan.
Collect (the "collect leg")
The first half of a payment: taking money out of the customer's mobile money wallet.
Settle / payout (the "settlement leg")
The second half of a payment: paying the money into the merchant's mobile money wallet (net of our fee). It's instant pass-through — we never hold it.
Cross-network
When the customer and the merchant are on different mobile money networks (e.g. customer on Vodacom, merchant on Airtel). Letting a merchant accept payment regardless of network is the core promise of our app.
Ledger
The app's precise internal record of every money movement — like a meticulous accounting book. It's the source of truth for what happened to every payment.
Double-entry bookkeeping
A centuries-old accounting method where every movement is recorded twice — money leaving one place and arriving in another — so the totals must always balance. If they don't, you know instantly there's an error. Standard for anything handling money.
Transaction state machine
A defined set of stages every payment must pass through — started, money taken, money sent, done, failed, refunded — with strict rules for moving between them. It guarantees a payment's status is never ambiguous or skipped.
Repeat-safeguard (idempotency keys)
A safeguard ensuring that if a payment request is accidentally sent twice — for example because the network hiccuped — the money still only moves once.
Reconciliation
Regularly comparing our records against the partner's records to make sure they match exactly, and flagging any difference. Our system does this automatically every day — a nightly audit.
Status message (webhook)
An automatic message the payment partner sends our server the instant a payment's status changes (for example, "money collected" or "delivery failed"), so our records stay current without us constantly asking.
Retry-with-backoff
Automatically re-attempting a failed message, waiting a little longer between each attempt, so brief glitches sort themselves out instead of becoming failures.
Operator approval prompt
The little menu that pops up on your phone from your mobile money operator asking you to enter your secret code to approve a payment. It comes from the operator, not from our app, and it works even on basic phones with no internet.
Sticker price & the merchant fee (MDR)
The customer always pays the exact displayed price — never a surcharge. Our fee (the "merchant discount rate", or MDR) is taken out of the merchant's side: they receive the price minus the fee. A 1% placeholder is used in mock-ups, but the real round-trip cost through the payment partner is ~3.5–5%, so the actual MDR is an open pricing decision (likely higher than 1%).
One-time code
The short code texted to your phone to prove the number is really yours, used when you sign up or reset your secret code.
Secret code
The secret code a user sets to unlock our app and approve payments inside it. (Separate from the operator's own code used in the approval prompt above.)
Identity verification (KYC)
Confirming who someone really is, as required by financial regulators. The paying customer's identity already rides on their SIM-card registration. For the merchant (our app's user), fuller business verification is flagged as an item to confirm with counsel — kept light for the MVP.
Pass-through model
Our chosen approach where the app never holds the money — it flows straight from the customer, through the payment partner, to the merchant in one motion (instant settlement). This keeps us simpler and (we believe) avoids needing our own financial license. Confirming that belief is the Week 1–4 legal gate.
Central bank (Banque Centrale du Congo)
The DRC's central bank and financial regulator — the body that issues financial licenses and sets the rules for handling money in the country.
Electronic money institution license
A specific, expensive type of financial license (requiring millions in capital) needed by companies that hold customers' money. Our pass-through model is designed to avoid needing this — but only the legal opinion can confirm it.
Cloud server / backend
The "brain" of the app that runs on rented computers in a data center (the "cloud") rather than on the user's phone. It talks to the payment partner, keeps the ledger, and does the real work. The phone app is just the friendly face on top.
One shared codebase
A single set of code that produces both the iPhone and Android versions of the app, instead of building each separately — faster and cheaper for a small team. (Built with a toolkit called React Native.)
Continuous integration
A system that automatically tests every code change the moment it's submitted, so mistakes are caught immediately rather than piling up.
Session tokens
Signed digital passes that keep a user securely logged in after they sign in, and which our server can revoke (for example, on sign-out) without needing the user's secret code.
Skeleton screens
Grey placeholder layouts shown while real content loads, making the app feel fast and responsive even on slow networks.
Unit tests / end-to-end tests
Automated checks for the code. Unit tests check one small piece in isolation; end-to-end tests run a whole flow (like a complete payment) from start to finish. Both run automatically to catch problems before users do.
Load testing
Simulating many people using the app at the same time to confirm it stays fast and doesn't break under real-world demand.
Structured logging & alerting
Recording events in an organized, searchable way (logging) and automatically pinging the team the instant something goes wrong (alerting), so problems are caught and traced quickly.
Translation system
A framework that swaps every piece of text in the app by language. Build it once, and adding a new language later means supplying translations rather than rebuilding screens.
Force-update control
The ability to require users to update to a new version — used sparingly, only when a serious fix must reach everyone quickly.
Code freeze
The point near the end where we deliberately stop adding new features and only fix bugs — locking the app down so it's stable and ready to launch. It's the finish line for this 16-week plan.
Dogfooding
Industry slang for "using your own product for real, every day" (from "eating your own dog food"). A powerful way to find bugs that planned testing misses.

What's next (after code freeze)

This plan ends with a finished, tested app ready to launch. The next phase — going live — takes about 4–8 more weeks and covers:

  • Switching from the test system to real money (the payment partner's live-money approval must be complete by here).
  • A small closed test with a first cluster of the ~40 gas stations + pop-up stores already lined up, taking real customer payments.
  • Publishing the merchant app to the Apple App Store and Google Play Store.
  • Public launch and the first real merchants accepting payments.

The deliberate advantage of stopping at code freeze: going live can begin when conditions are right (legal cleared, payment partner approved, team ready) rather than on a fixed calendar date. See the full roadmap for the complete arc.
