Who actually competes with us

Created 2026-07-01 · Re-verified the same day by a dedicated fact-checking pass (research agents against live primary sources). Three things changed from the first draft: the market shares, Wave's status, and the claim that "nobody does what we do" - which was too strong.
The short answer: most names people call our "competitors" are not. Some are suppliers we pay (pawaPay). Some are the mobile networks we tie together. The honest picture after re-checking: the mechanics of what we do already exist in the DRC - at least one approved player (FlexPay) lets a merchant accept payment from any network without the customer installing anything - but nobody has ever seriously contested the small-merchant market with it. The segment is unclaimed, not proven lost. Our opening is not "nobody can do this"; it is "nobody has tried to win small merchants" - and we start with a committed launch network (~50 fuel stations) that the closest rival never built. One giant is at the door, though: Wave has a Kinshasa company, a bank partner, and published DRC terms - not launched yet, but coming.

What we do, in one sentence: a merchant gets one till that accepts money from a customer on any mobile-money network; the customer needs no app and no internet (they dial a code or scan a QR and approve on their own phone); the merchant is paid instantly, and we never hold the money.

1 · The plumbing - suppliers, not rivals

These companies move money between networks on behalf of businesses. A shopper never sees them. We rent one (pawaPay) the way a toaster maker buys electricity - they are suppliers, not competitors.

  • pawaPay - our supplier. Covers 3 of the 4 DRC networks (not Afrimoney). Its published fee: a flat 1% plus each network's own cut, adding up to ~3.5-5% for a full cross-network payment. Re-verified on its live fees page 2026-07-01: unchanged. Because we rent, that price is a cost we don't control - the whole profitability plan is about lowering it.
  • MOKO Afrika - the natural fallback; its API docs list all four networks including Afrimoney (verified 2026-07-01). Pricing not public.
  • DPO Pay and Onafriq - heavier, enterprise rails; contract-only pricing. One correction from the first draft: Onafriq did not "launch Visa Pay" - Visa launched its Visa Pay consumer wallet in the DRC in 2025, and Onafriq supplies the mobile-money plumbing behind it. Visa Pay is live in 2026.

2 · The mobile networks - the walls we bridge

Each network's money service works well only inside its own bubble, and each offers its own merchant tools. The latest regulator figures (ARPTC report for 2025, published April 2026) redraw the map from our first draft:

NetworkShare of DRC mobile moneyNote
Vodacom M-Pesa~44%Still #1, no longer dominant (was quoted as ~51%)
Airtel Money~41%Nearly caught M-Pesa; 2025 revenue gap closed to ~$12M
Orange Money~14%Smallest of the three, fastest user growth (+50% in a year)
Afrimoney<1%A coverage footnote, not a factor

Why this matters to us: a near-even three-way race makes cross-network acceptance more valuable, not less - the chance that a random customer and merchant sit on the same network keeps falling, and no single network's till solves a shop's problem. The networks compete with us only in the narrow sense that a shop could settle for one network and turn away everyone else.

3 · The real rivals - the claim we had to correct

Correction (2026-07-01): the first draft said no company offers what we offer. Fact-checking proved that too strong. The mechanics exist today; the execution for small merchants is the open ground.
  • FlexPay, relaunched as "FlexPaie" (INFOSET) - the closest thing to us that already exists, profiled in depth (flexpay.md). BCC-authorized aggregator since 2020; holds no funds (like us); the paying customer needs no app - they get a prompt from their own operator and enter their PIN (our exact flow); covers all four networks plus cards. Read its history correctly, though: after ~6 years there are no public adoption numbers anywhere (its own relaunch coverage cites none) and its merchant app shows ~1K+ downloads - but this is not a failed attempt at our market. FlexPaie is an IT group's payment gateway serving its own e-commerce, school-fees, and ticketing platforms, aimed at online and formal collections (churches, events, rent, utility and supermarket partners). It never seriously attempted informal merchants. So it proves our mechanics are feasible and leaves our segment unclaimed rather than proven lost - which is better for us than a failure would be, since a real failure would suggest merchants won't pay. Watch for: a published fee schedule, or any move toward street-level merchant acquisition (if it ever competes for accounts, expect it to court formal ones - like fuel-station chains - through enterprise deals).
  • CinetPay RDC - sells an in-store terminal ("TPE") that collects payments "whatever the source." Aimed at shops; aggregates the networks.
  • MaishaPay - a "Terminal" product accepting M-Pesa, Airtel Money, Orange Money and cards in person; its small-merchant QR mechanics are unclear.
  • The networks' own merchant tools - the most realistic everyday pressure: a shop happy to be paid on one network only doesn't need anyone above it.

What this means: we cannot win on "only we can do this." We can win on doing it well for small merchants: onboarding, the no-app customer flow, free same-network recording, instant confirmation, clean records and reconciliation - plus actually showing up where merchants are. Nobody has visibly done that yet.

4 · Consumer wallets - same word, different customer

IllicoCash (Rawbank's wallet) is the DRC product most often pointed at as our competitor. It is polished and bank-backed, and it has a merchant QR - but it is a closed wallet: to pay that QR, the customer must already be an IllicoCash user with money loaded in it. Our model is the opposite - the customer pays from whatever balance they already have, on any network, with nothing to install. IllicoCash sells to the shopper; we sell to the shop. (Details: inbound international transfers require its upgraded "Premium" tier; the often-quoted French-plus-Kituba language support comes from press materials, while its own website runs French/English.) The three networks' apps are consumer wallets in the same sense.

5 · Incoming: Wave

Status change (2026-07-01). The first draft filed Wave under "foreign look-alikes, not in the DRC." That is now wrong in the ways that matter: Wave has a company registered in Kinshasa (since 2024), a partnership under which Ecobank RDC issues the e-money with Wave as distributor, and live DRC terms of use on wave.com. No confirmed commercial launch as of 2026-07-01 - but this is a funded giant with Africa's best low-fee consumer playbook standing at the door. If it launches, the consumer-wallet landscape changes overnight, and its merchant-acceptance side becomes the thing to watch. Track it as a named risk.

The other foreign models we studied (M-Pesa Kenya, OPay/PalmPay in Nigeria) remain what they were: lessons on growth and product sequencing, not DRC rivals. Write-ups live in the comparables research.

Our opening, honestly stated

The gap in the DRC market is no longer "cross-network acceptance is impossible" - approved players can already do the mechanics, and the regulator wants interop to exist. The gap is that no one has packaged it into a product small merchants actually adopt, or even seriously tried: one till for every network, a customer flow with no app and no internet, instant confirmation the merchant can trust, records that double as bookkeeping, and feet-on-the-street onboarding. That is the product we are building - and we do not start from zero. The beta launches into a committed network of ~50 fuel stations: real, signed distribution that FlexPay never built in six years, in a high-frequency, cash-heavy vertical that gives us day-one volume to measure retention, the same-network mix, and willingness to pay. Two honest cautions: fuel stations sit at the formal end of the market - exactly the account type an enterprise player could court - and commitments are not usage; the advantage is realized by converting those stations into habitual use during the beta, not by holding the list.

The biggest strategic exposure is still not a named competitor - it is that we rent our rails: pawaPay (or the networks) set our costs, see our volume, and could move up into the merchant layer. The answer to that is the regulatory path: our own authorization and direct network deals.
