Who actually competes with us

Created 2026-07-01 · A plain-language map of the companies people think are our competitors, and which ones really are. Built from the aggregator and comparables findings, plus fresh web research on IllicoCash.
The short answer: most of the names that get called our "competitors" are not competing with us at all. Some are suppliers we would pay to move money (pawaPay and its rivals). Some are the mobile networks themselves - the very thing we tie together. Some are consumer wallets (like IllicoCash) that serve the shopper, not the shop. The handful that genuinely overlap with us are few, and none of them does exactly what we do: let a merchant accept a payment from a customer on any network, with the customer needing no app and no internet.
What we do, in one sentence. A merchant gets one till that accepts money from a customer on any mobile-money network (Vodacom M-Pesa, Airtel, Orange). The customer pays by dialing a code or scanning a QR - no app, no smartphone needed - and the merchant is paid instantly, minus our fee. We never hold the money; it passes straight through us.

The rest of this page explains each group in plain language.

1 · The plumbing (rails) - our suppliers, not our rivals

These companies do one thing: move money between mobile-money networks on behalf of a business. A shopper never hears their name. Think of them as the pipes and wiring. We are building the appliance that plugs into the wall. Renting one of these is exactly our plan - we chose pawaPay. So calling them competitors is like calling the electricity company a competitor to a toaster maker.

pawaPay ← our chosen supplier

Supplier
Mobile-money-only rails across 20+ African countries.
  • Why it's not a competitor: it has no shop-facing product - no app a merchant would use, no till, no receipts. It sells the money movement; we sell the merchant experience on top.
  • Why we picked it: it's the only option with public, self-serve pricing and a sandbox you can test in minutes. Covers 3 of the 4 DRC networks (all but Afrimoney). Its fee is a flat 1% plus the network's own cut.
  • The catch we depend on them for: because we rent their rails, their price (~3.5%-5% for a round-trip cross-network payment) is a cost we don't control. That is exactly why an alternative to "pay pawaPay per transaction" is worth exploring next.

MOKO Afrika

Supplier
DRC-focused rails - the only one we found that covers all four networks, including Afrimoney.
  • Why it matters: it's the natural fallback supplier if reaching Afrimoney users ever becomes important (pawaPay can't).
  • Why it's not a competitor: same as pawaPay - it's plumbing, not a merchant product. Its pricing isn't public, so we'd have to ask for a quote.

DPO Pay  ·  Onafriq

Suppliers
Two more rails options, both heavier and more "enterprise."
  • DPO Pay - a card-first payment gateway (owned by a big regional bank group). Good at cards and online checkout; its real-time payout side is less clear. Aimed at larger merchants via a sales contract.
  • Onafriq (formerly MFS Africa) - giant cross-border rails that mainly serve banks and other payment companies, not small shops. It's structurally the closest to "a company like us at wholesale scale," but it sells to institutions, not merchants. In late 2025 it launched Visa Pay in the DRC.
  • Why neither competes: both are suppliers you'd contract with, not products a Kinshasa fuel station would use directly. Neither publishes DRC pricing.

2 · The mobile networks - the walls we bridge

Vodacom M-Pesa, Airtel Money, and Orange Money are the phone companies' own money services (roughly 51% / 31% / 17% of DRC mobile money; Afrimoney is under 1%). Each already lets merchants get paid - but only by customers on the same network. They are walled gardens: cross-network payments between them are limited and clunky, even where a bit of interoperability exists.

This is the wall we knock down. With us, a merchant puts up one till and gets paid no matter which network the customer is on. So the networks overlap with us only in the narrow sense that each offers merchant acceptance inside its own bubble. Our whole reason to exist is that a shop doesn't want three separate tills and three separate reconciliations.

Afrimoney (Africell) is a real network but tiny (<1% share) and our main supplier (pawaPay) can't reach it. So it's neither a meaningful competitor nor, for now, a channel - it's a coverage gap to note, not worry about.

3 · Consumer wallets - same word, different customer

These are apps a shopper installs to hold money and pay. They get called competitors because some of them also let a merchant take a payment. But they are built for the person spending money, not the shop collecting it - a fundamentally different customer.

IllicoCash (by Rawbank, a major DRC bank)

Overlaps at the edge
A polished, bank-backed "super-wallet" - probably the DRC product most likely to be pointed at and called our competitor.
  • What it does: send money anywhere in the DRC, receive from abroad (it was the first DRC wallet to do cross-border mobile-money transfers), top up airtime, pay bills, buy a virtual Visa/Mastercard, cash out at agents or ATMs, and - the overlapping bit - scan a QR to pay a merchant. Available in French and Kituba (Kikongo).
  • Why it looks like a competitor: it has merchant QR payments, so a shop can get paid through it.
  • Why it really isn't (the key difference): IllicoCash is a closed wallet - to pay a merchant, the customer must be an IllicoCash user with money loaded in that wallet. Our model is the opposite: the customer pays from whatever they already have (their M-Pesa, Airtel, or Orange balance), with no new app to download. IllicoCash grows a shop's reach only among IllicoCash users; we give a shop reach across everyone with any mobile-money account.
  • Different customer: IllicoCash sells to the shopper and treats merchant acceptance as a side feature. We sell to the merchant and make cross-network acceptance the whole product.

The three network apps (the M-Pesa / Airtel / Orange apps) are also consumer wallets in this sense - same story as above.

4 · Foreign look-alikes - teachers, not rivals

We studied a set of big fintechs from other countries to learn how they grew, made money, and rolled out features. They are not in the DRC merchant market, so they don't compete with us - they're reference points.

  • Wave (Senegal / Ivory Coast) - the closest model to ours in spirit: dead-simple app, QR, low fees, big agent network. Our design borrows its "one clear action per screen" feel. Not present in the DRC.
  • M-Pesa (Kenya) - the textbook story of mobile money growing into a full super-app with lending. A model for where we could go later, not a rival here.
  • OPay / PalmPay (Nigeria) - agent-heavy super-apps that grew fast on incentives. Lessons on growth, not competitors.

Full write-ups live in the comparables research and the synthesis report.

So who really competes with us?

Being honest about it, the field of true rivals is thin:

  • The networks' own merchant tools - if a shop is happy taking payment on just one network (say only M-Pesa), it doesn't need us. Our pitch beats this only because most shops want to accept everyone. This is the most realistic "why wouldn't a merchant use us" pressure.
  • Anyone who copies our exact model - a DRC-focused merchant app that accepts all networks with no customer app. The pieces to build it are available to others (the same rails we rent - pawaPay, MOKO). Today we didn't find a company doing precisely this and marketing it to small merchants, which is our opening. But nothing stops a rail company (or IllicoCash) from moving into this space.
  • IllicoCash, if it pushed harder into open, network-agnostic merchant acceptance - it has the bank, the brand, and a head start on consumers. Worth watching, not fearing today.
The real risk isn't a competitor - it's a supplier. Because we rent our rails, our biggest strategic exposure is that pawaPay (or the networks behind it) sets the price and could one day offer the merchant product themselves. That, not IllicoCash, is the reason to explore alternatives to paying per transaction.

Our niche, stated plainly

We are the only one selling this exact thing to DRC merchants: one till that takes money from a customer on any network, where the customer needs no app and no internet (they just dial a code or scan a QR and approve on their own phone), the merchant absorbs the fee so the customer pays the sticker price, and the money passes straight through to the merchant instantly - we never hold it.

The rails companies do the plumbing but have no shop-facing product. The networks each cover only their own bubble. The consumer wallets serve the shopper and need the shopper to join them first. We sit in the gap all of them leave open: network-agnostic, app-free, merchant-first acceptance in the DRC.
