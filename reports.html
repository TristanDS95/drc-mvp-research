Research & reports — summary

Updated 2026-06-09 · A plain-English digest of every research phase, newest at the bottom. Each section links to the full write-up. Sources for every claim live in the linked files and 04-sources/sources.md.
This is the short navigational digest. For the full detailed report see report.html; for how the field of competitors sorts out see competitor-landscape.html.
This project set out to answer one question — can we build a DRC cross-network payment app on rented rails, and how? — and the answer became yes, on pawaPay. Along the way the work widened into product design, an architecture bake-off, and a study of other fintechs — and the product itself pivoted to a merchant-acquiring app (a customer pays a merchant; USSD is a first-class channel). This page is the running summary of that journey.
How to read this: sections are ordered oldest first, newest last — scroll to the bottom for the most recent research (the architecture comparison and the comparative-fintech study). Each phase shows the question it answered, the key findings, the decision it produced, and links to the detail.
1
Can we even build it, and how?
Feasibility research · the original question
done

What we found

  • Operators: Airtel has the most developer-friendly API (self-serve sandbox, OAuth); M-Pesa is full-featured but partner-gated; Orange's public API is collection-only; Afrimoney has <1% share and no public developer portal. None publish their API fees.
  • Aggregators ("rented rails"): pawaPay covers 3 of the 4 operators (M-Pesa, Airtel, Orange) with published per-leg pricing and a self-serve sandbox. MOKO is the only one covering all 4 (incl. Afrimoney); DPO is cards-first; Onafriq is a PSP-to-PSP hub.
  • Cost: ~3.5–5% per round-trip via pawaPay (before currency exchange).
  • Paying someone: by phone number (MSISDN) — there is no national QR standard in the DRC. KYC rides the user's existing SIM registration.
Decision Build the MVP on pawaPay, covering M-Pesa + Airtel + Orange (~99% of users). (Direction since updated — see the pivot below: the MVP is now a merchant-acquiring app, so merchant onboarding is in scope and addressing is by merchant till / QR, with a charge-by-customer-number fallback.) Open a separate legal/licensing track.

Should we build our own rails instead of renting?
Build-vs-rent + Hub2 check
done

What we found

  • Building our own aggregator is technically feasible but strategically wrong for v1: it needs four separate operator contracts (commercially hard), a BCC licence with US$2.5M minimum capital (regulatory gate), and it only saves pawaPay's ~1% margin — not the operator fees we'd still pay.
  • Hub2 (a Francophone-Africa aggregator) is not live in the DRC — worth re-checking in 12–24 months, not now.
Decision Rent now (pawaPay); consider owning rails only much later (hybrid v3+), once volume justifies it.

How will the app actually work?
Product spec
done

What we decided

  • One merchant-acceptance flow — a customer pays a registered merchant; primary path is customer-initiated USSD (scan the merchant's QR / dial the till), with a charge-by-customer-number fallback. Pure pass-through, instant settlement (we never hold funds). The customer pays the exact sticker price; the merchant absorbs our fee (MDR) and approves on their own operator PIN prompt (no customer app). Merchant onboarding (settlement + till) is in scope.
  • Stack: React Native + Expo (merchant app), AWS Cape Town, Postgres + Redis, Africa's Talking (SMS) + Smile ID (KYC, later) + Sentry + PostHog. Backend built in Python / FastAPI; the final language call was left to the hired engineer.
Output A full spec: functionality, UI, screens, data model (Merchant / Transaction / LedgerEntry), API + USSD contracts.

4
Can it work without internet?
Offline / feature-phone (USSD) feasibility
done

What we found

  • A phone cannot "be pawaPay" — bridging networks needs a server that briefly holds funds. But the operators already do cross-network sends over USSD, so an offline app could drive those existing menus (Android-only, fragile, no fee capture).
  • It's a convenience layer, not a payments business on its own — best as a phased channel.
Decision (later updated) Originally "v2 wedge." Now MVP-critical: in the merchant-acquiring pivot, customer-initiated USSD is the customer's primary way to pay (no app), built in the MVP — not a later phase. The audience is no longer limited to urban smartphone users.

5
Our design vs the merchant-QR brief
Architecture comparison — verdict since reversed
done

What we found

  • The two were different products: the design at the time was a consumer cross-network pass-through (collect + pay out, no merchant sign-up); the brief was a merchant-acquiring QR system (collection into a merchant, merchants onboard). The original write-up scored the dimensions in favour of the consumer model.
  • This verdict has since been reversed. The product pivoted to the merchant-acquiring model: merchant onboarding, a QR / till the customer scans or dials, and customer-initiated USSD are now exactly what we build. The plumbing both designs share (collect → settle on pawaPay, double-entry ledger, reconciliation) carried over unchanged.
  • Adopted from the merchant brief: a separate webhook service, an operator status-code lookup table, and a 5-question aggregator-vetting checklist.
It also caught real bugs in the spec (now fixed): pawaPay signs callbacks with RFC-9421 public-key signatures, not HMAC; AWS App Runner isn't available in Cape Town (use Fargate); and our latency figure was optimistic. Also: Flutterwave doesn't actually serve DRC mobile money (the brief's claim was wrong).
Decision (updated) Adopt the merchant-acquiring / QR product model — it is the MVP. Keep the shared pass-through plumbing and the borrowed webhook / status-code / vetting ideas.

6
What can we learn from other fintechs?
Comparative research · most recent
done

What we found (8 companies: M-Pesa, Wave, OPay/PalmPay, MTN/Airtel, Chipper/Flutterwave, Onafriq, bKash, global super-apps)

  • The big lesson: moving money earns almost nothing — it wins customers and gathers data. The money is in what comes after: merchant acceptance → lending → wealth/insurance. The pivot puts us at that first earner — merchant acceptance — from day one.
  • Our gap: pass-through forgoes the other usual earners (float, cash-out fees, agent networks). The earner we do have on day one is the MDR charged to merchants on each accepted payment.
  • Our realistic levers: the MDR on merchant acceptance (the core earner, and now the MVP itself), the US$↔CDF currency-exchange margin (the one adjacency we uniquely have), and capital-light lending later via a partner bank (M-Pesa/bKash pattern).
  • Feature-phone/USSD is proven first-class: M-Pesa ran 14 years before its app and is still mostly USSD; bKash ran USSD-only for ~7 years. Our customers pay by USSD with no app — it's the MVP's primary customer channel.
  • Biggest risk: we depend on the very operators we compete with — when Wave undercut Orange, Orange blocked Wave from selling its airtime for a year.
Scope change The MVP is merchant-acquiring; merchant acquisition (the ~40 gas stations + pop-up stores) is the go-to-market; customer-initiated USSD is an MVP-critical channel; the audience is not limited to urban smartphone users; comparative product-strategy research is in scope.

What's next upcoming

Research and the product spec are in good shape, and the build of the merchant app has begun (Python/FastAPI backend with the ledger / merchants / transactions domains and HTTP + USSD channels). Open questions worth resolving in parallel: the MDR pricing decision (1% placeholder is below the ~3.5–5% pawaPay cost), the legal/licensing posture (BCC), a real per-leg price check with pawaPay, and the per-operator USSD shortcode lead times for the ~40-gas-station rollout.
