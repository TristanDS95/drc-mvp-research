The path to profitability

Created 2026-07-01 · Rewritten later that day after a primary-source regulatory review (regulatory landscape) and a fact-checking pass with research agents. All numbers below were re-verified against live sources on 2026-07-01. Claims are tagged: research = traced to a verified source · stance = the product owner's decision · open = not known yet.
The short answer: the MVP loses a little money on every payment, on purpose - its job is to prove merchants want cross-network acceptance, not to earn. Profit comes later, from cutting the fees we pay, and the route is now concrete: get the central bank's lightweight aggregator permit, deal directly with the mobile networks to remove our middleman's markup, and only then decide what price merchants will bear. The old idea of holding customer money ourselves (the "treasury" model) is retired - it needs the market's heaviest licence for a benefit we can mostly get without it. The one question that decides everything is unchanged and unanswered: will merchants pay?

Today: losing money on purpose

The app is a pure pass-through. A customer pays, the money moves over rented rails (pawaPay), the merchant receives it, and we never hold it. We charge the merchant exactly what the payment costs us - so we keep nothing - and same-network payments are free. research (ADRs 0003/0007/0009)

That is deliberate. We can't outspend funded rivals, so the MVP buys the one thing money can't rent: proof that real merchants adopt this and keep using it. Only with that volume do we have anything to negotiate with. stance

Where the money goes

A cross-network payment is really two operations: collect from the customer's network, pay out to the merchant's. We pay a fee on both legs - the network's cut plus pawaPay's flat 1% per leg.

A refunded payment is worse: the refund is billed like a payout and the original collection fee isn't returned, so a failed-then-refunded payment loses roughly 4-5% of its value (our arithmetic from published rates - pawaPay publishes the parts, not the total).

Why we can't just add a fee on top

Three prices box us in: research

  • Rivals charge 2-3%. Comparable payment acquirers in Africa publish ~2% (Ghana) to ~2.9% (Kenya) for mobile-money collections (Flutterwave's live pricing, checked 2026-07-01).
  • Cash charges 0%.
  • The customer can bypass us for 1%. Orange's published tariff for sending to another network is a flat 1% (it requires an upgraded-KYC account, which is real friction - but the price is real too).

Our cost is 3.5-5% before we earn a cent. So profit cannot come from stacking a margin on top of today's costs - it must come from lowering the costs.

The path: three steps

Step 1 - Prove it (now)

The MVP

Run the beta on rented rails, unprofitable, and measure three things: do merchants keep using it, what share of payments is same-network, and what will merchants pay (see the hinge). The beta launches into a committed network of ~50 fuel stations - real distribution in a high-frequency, cash-heavy vertical, and the asset our closest rival never built (see the competitor landscape). stance

Step 2 - Become a real DRC player

Light paperwork

Register a Congolese company and obtain the central bank's aggregator permit: a document dossier, a 60-day decision clock, no stated capital minimum - the same permit pawaPay itself operates on in the DRC. Then integrate directly with M-Pesa and Airtel (together ~85% of the market), keeping pawaPay as backup. This removes pawaPay's ~2% share of the round trip - the only part of the fee we can remove without holding money. research (regulatory landscape; licensing-categories.md)

Step 3 - Set the price merchants will pay

The business

With costs cut to roughly the networks' own fees, choose the model the beta's data supports: a small per-payment fee, a flat monthly subscription, or free payments with paid merchant tools. This is a pricing decision, not an engineering one - and it is gated entirely on willingness to pay. stance

A possible shortcut to watch, not to plan on: the state is building a national payment switch ("Mosolo") that is supposed to make cross-network payments cheap for everyone - and as a permitted aggregator we would be entitled to plug in. But it has been "launched" twice since 2020 without evidence it works for mobile money, so we track it with hard indicators (its governing body legally created, real prices published) and keep it out of the cost model. open (national-switch-interoperability.md)

What we dropped, and why: the treasury / float idea

The earlier plan's "bigger lever" was to hold a money balance on every network and settle payments internally, only occasionally moving the net difference across networks. It would cut fees - but the fact-checked reality killed it: research

  • Holding money = the market's heaviest licence. An e-money licence in the DRC requires ~$2.5M of paid-up capital, and the central bank's own register (checked directly, 2026-07-01) shows exactly four licence holders - all subsidiaries of the phone networks. No independent company has ever gotten one.
  • The networks would fight it. Netting quietly removes their cross-network revenue; their anti-fraud systems freeze accounts that behave like unlicensed processors.
  • Its main benefit may arrive for free. If the national switch ever works, it nets cross-network flows centrally for all participants - no custody needed.

A middle option exists on paper - a "payment institution" permit that may allow briefly holding funds in transit at far lower capital - but its capital requirement is not published. That is the #1 question for DRC counsel, and nothing before Step 3 needs the answer. open

Same-network payments stay free

When customer and merchant are on the same network, the customer pays the merchant directly and we just record and confirm it - it costs us nothing and wins merchants. Under the old treasury idea this free traffic was a "leak" to be captured someday; with that idea retired, the answer is simple: it stays free, permanently, as our cheapest acquisition tool. The beta measures how big this slice really is. stance

The hinge: will merchants pay?

We have no evidence for this number - and it decides everything. Merchant willingness-to-pay in the DRC has never been researched (it was deliberately out of the research scope). The squeeze is real: rivals benchmark at 2-3%, cash is free, a determined customer can send off-net for 1%, and at least one approved competitor (FlexPay) can already do the mechanics - though it has never contested the small-merchant segment, so no one has ever actually tested whether these merchants will pay (see who actually competes with us). Whatever we charge has to survive all of that. open
  • Per-payment fee: will a shop absorb a few percent when cash is free? Unknown.
  • Subscription: will an informal DRC merchant pay a flat monthly fee for the till, records, and reach (the pattern that worked for Paytm in India)? No DRC precedent either way; it may only fit the formal segment (fuel stations, chains). open

What the beta must prove

  1. Adoption is real - merchants keep using it after the novelty wears off.
  2. The same-network vs cross-network mix - measured from day one (the app already tags each payment).
  3. A real price signal - put an actual fee or subscription in front of beta merchants and watch what they do, before building more.
  4. A legal read - DRC counsel on two narrow questions: does the current model already need the aggregator permit, and what capital does a "payment institution" permit require. stance
Bottom line. Profitability is a climb, not a switch. Stay lean and unprofitable through the MVP to earn adoption; take the light permit and go direct to the networks to cut costs; keep holding-money off the table; and let the beta answer the only question that matters - what merchants will pay.
