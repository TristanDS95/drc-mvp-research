DRC payment app — development roadmap

Date: 2026-06-04 · Team size: 2–4 · MVP target: merchant acceptance — ~40 gas stations + pop-up stores already lined up · Path: rented rails (pawaPay) first, hybrid later

Overview & assumptions

Seven phases, two horizons. Phases 0–3 are the MVP arc on rented rails — about 6–11 months elapsed from kickoff to a public beta. Phases 4–6 are the "own the rails" track — a multi-year ambition, not part of v1. Time ranges assume a 2–4 person team and reflect realistic slippage; the low end requires a tight 4-person team with clean execution, the high end accommodates a 2-person team or normal startup friction.

Scope reminders (from research)

  • In scope (v1): a merchant app (dashboard + onboarding); customer-initiated USSD payment (scan the merchant's QR — a tel: USSD dial-through — or dial the till; no customer app, PIN via the operator's own prompt) with a merchant-initiated "charge by customer number" fallback; merchant onboarding (settlement account + till / short code; KYC flagged); QR codes (central); collect → bridge → settle via pawaPay; M-Pesa + Airtel + Orange (~99% of DRC mobile money users); CDF + USD; French primary UI; MSISDN-based addressing.
  • Out of scope (v1): bank-account settlement, rewards / loyalty, bill pay, a separate consumer app, Afrimoney (defer to v2 via MOKO Afrika if material), card payments, direct operator integration.
  • Beachhead: ~40 gas stations + pop-up stores already lined up — merchant acquisition (usually the hardest part of a new acquiring product) is substantially de-risked for v1.
  • Wedge: merchant acceptance — the customer pays the exact sticker price and the merchant absorbs our fee (MDR); instant pass-through settlement to the merchant's existing mobile money account (we never hold funds).

Phase 0   Foundation & due diligence

4–8 weeks elapsed

Goal: have everything non-engineering in place so Phase 1 starts with no blockers.

Workstreams

Legal Counsel engagement

  • Engage a DRC-registered fintech / banking counsel (1–2 weeks to select).
  • Settle the partner-PSP question: does the MVP entity operate under pawaPay's BCC posture, or do we need a licensed local-partner arrangement? (per KYC research)
  • Stand up entity structure (DRC subsidiary, or operating-from-abroad model) per counsel.

Ops Vendor signups

  • pawaPay sandbox signup at dashboard.sandbox.pawapay.io (hours).
  • Open production-KYC questionnaire (plans page) — paperwork can take 2–4 weeks running in parallel with build.
  • Apple Developer + Google Play Console accounts.
  • Optional: Smile ID account for app-side end-user KYC if held-balance path is adopted later.

Eng Architecture design

  • Choose stack: merchant app (React Native vs Flutter vs native pair) + web dashboard, backend (Python/FastAPI as built, or Node/Go; Postgres, Redis), hosting (AWS / GCP region near DRC — Europe / Cape Town are the viable options).
  • Design the core domains — ledger, merchants, transactions — with a double-entry ledger and a transaction state machine: idempotency keys, retry policy, terminal-vs-pending states, webhook handling.
  • Design the two payment channels: customer-initiated USSD (QR / till dial-through, PIN on the operator's own prompt) and the merchant-initiated "charge by customer number" push.
  • Operator inference logic: MSISDN-prefix lookup; manual override UX.
  • Threat model and security baseline (TLS, secret management, OWASP ASVS Level 2 as a target).

PM Brand & positioning

  • Provisional name, domain, French primary copy.
  • One-page user-flow doc agreed by team.
  • Set up project management (Linear / Notion / shared docs).

Team allocation (typical 3-person team)

Founder/PM: Legal, vendor signups, brand — full time
Backend eng: Architecture design, stack POCs — full time
Mobile eng: Mobile-stack POC, design sync with founder — full time

Exit criteria

  • Counsel engaged, written opinion on entity / licensing posture.
  • pawaPay sandbox account active; production KYC submitted.
  • Architecture doc signed off; stack choices made.
  • Provisional brand in place; first end-to-end mock screens.

Phase 1   MVP build (sandbox)

12–20 weeks elapsed (~3–5 months)

Goal: a working end-to-end merchant-acceptance app on pawaPay sandbox — a customer pays a registered merchant (via the USSD / QR channel) at the exact sticker price, funds collect → bridge → settle to the merchant, and the merchant sees it on their dashboard. Demoable to friends acting as customers.

Backend workstreams

  • Merchant auth & identity — merchant signup/login, JWT session, Merchant entity (settlement MSISDN, till / short code; DRC +243 validation). KYC flagged.
  • Ledger & transactions — double-entry ledger (accounts: customer:external, merchant:external, pawapay:clearing, revenue:fees); Transaction + LedgerEntry entities; transaction state machine (initiated → collection-pending → collection-confirmed → settlement-pending → settlement-confirmed / failed); idempotency keys per request.
  • Payment codes & USSD channel — generate per-merchant payment codes / till routing; the customer-initiated USSD dial-through (QR encodes a tel: USSD string, e.g. tel:*123*1001%23) and the merchant-initiated "charge by customer number" push.
  • pawaPay integration — collection (Deposit) + settlement payout (Disbursement) + status query; webhook receiver with signature verification; retry/backoff. (Webhook receiver + reconciliation land in Phase D of the build.)
  • Operator inference — MSISDN prefix → operator map; configurable via DB row so we can update without redeploy.
  • Currency handling — CDF + USD; per-operator decimal precision (per pawaPay docs: Vodacom CDF no decimals, others 2 decimals).
  • Reconciliation job — daily compare of our ledger against pawaPay's status feed; flag discrepancies.
  • Observability — structured logging, error tracking (Sentry), metrics (transaction success rate, latency by leg).
  • Admin tooling — internal dashboard to inspect merchants and transactions and force reconciliation.

Merchant app workstreams

  • Merchant onboarding — register the merchant, settlement details, and a till / short code; generate the merchant's QR (a tel: USSD dial-through) for display at the point of sale.
  • Accept flow — merchant enters/sees the amount; customer pays at the exact sticker price by scanning the QR or dialling the till; PIN is entered on the customer's own operator prompt. Merchant absorbs the fee (MDR); dashboard shows pending → settled and net-of-fee.
  • Charge-by-customer-number fallback — merchant keys the customer's number to push a collection prompt to that customer's phone.
  • Transaction history & reconciliation view — list, detail, retry-on-failure, daily totals.
  • Localisation infrastructure — French primary; structure ready for Lingala / Swahili in Phase 3.
  • Telemetry — funnel analytics, crash reporting.
  • Build / release pipeline — TestFlight + Play Console internal testing.

Quality bars before exit

  • 100 sandbox merchant-acceptance round-trips with >= 99% terminal-state convergence (success or known-failure, no stuck-pending).
  • Customer-initiated USSD path proven end-to-end against the sandbox (QR / till dial-through → collection → settlement to merchant).
  • Idempotency proven (replay the same request → no duplicate operator call).
  • Webhook failure simulation works (drop callbacks → reconciliation job converges).
  • Manual operator override works for ambiguous prefixes.
  • French copy reviewed by a native French speaker (ideally DRC-French).

Founder/PM: Merchant testing (gas-station pilots), copy, partner / legal / operator-shortcode pursuit in parallel
Backend eng: Ledger + merchant model + pawaPay integration + USSD channel + admin tools

Phase 2   Production launch (closed → public beta)

4–8 weeks elapsed

Goal: real DRC merchants accepting real customer payments through the app, observed end-to-end, with on-call coverage.

Workstreams

Ops pawaPay production

  • Complete pawaPay production KYC (2–4 weeks elapsed, depends on them).
  • Production API tokens; switch endpoints from sandbox to prod.
  • Fund operator-side balances per pawaPay's treasury guidance.
  • Settlement-to-bank cadence agreed.

Eng Production readiness

  • Infrastructure-as-code, CI/CD, rollback strategy.
  • Production observability dashboards (success rate by operator, leg latencies, error categories).
  • On-call rotation with PagerDuty / OpsGenie.
  • Database backups, secrets rotation.

PM Beta program

  • Closed beta with a first cluster of the ~40 gas stations + pop-up stores already lined up — week 1–3.
  • Merchant-support intake — initially a phone number + WhatsApp.
  • Iterate on real-world failure modes (the Tanzania M-Pesa "undocumented rate limits at scale" pattern).

UX Store submission

  • App Store screenshots, privacy disclosures, store copy in French + English.
  • Google Play submission (review 1–3 days typical).
  • App Store submission (review 1–7 days; financial apps occasionally longer).

Quality bars before opening to public

  • 100 real-world merchant-acceptance transactions across all three operators (M-Pesa, Airtel, Orange) with >= 95% success terminal rate.
  • Mean reconciliation lag < 1 hour; every settlement reaches the merchant's account.
  • One end-to-end merchant-support cycle (issue raised → resolved → ledger reconciled) completed cleanly.
  • Crash-free sessions > 99% across both platforms.

Team allocation

Founder/PM: Merchant beta onboarding, support, store copy
Backend eng: Production hardening, on-call setup
Mobile eng: Store submissions, polish, telemetry tuning

Phase 3   Scale & polish (post-launch)

12–24 weeks elapsed (ongoing thereafter)

Goal: the app is the better way for a merchant to accept cross-network mobile money than the alternatives; merchant count and volume grow; the system handles it without us touching the firefighting button each day.

Engineering tracks

  • Real-world telemetry → ranked backlog. Once 1,000+ transactions/day are live, the failure-mode list reorganises priorities. Track success rate per (customer-operator, merchant-operator) pair; chase the worst ones first.
  • Localisation: Lingala + Swahili UI strings (+4–6 weeks). Native speakers required for review.
  • Afrimoney coverage via MOKO Afrika if commercially material — 4–8 weeks integration on top of pawaPay; routing layer chooses path per operator.
  • Performance hardening: webhook redundancy, idempotency-key stress test, retry strategy tuned for DRC mobile network conditions.
  • Fraud / abuse signals: rate-limit per merchant + customer number + device; anomaly detection on amount / frequency.

Growth tracks

  • Merchant referral mechanic: reward existing merchants for referring other merchants; treat merchant-to-merchant referral as a primary acquisition channel alongside direct sales.
  • Funnel analytics: merchant signup → onboarded (settlement set) → first accepted payment → repeat-acceptance conversion; identify drop-offs.
  • Merchant-support tooling: ticketing system; canned responses in French; SLA targets.

Compliance & legal tracks (often the quiet long-pole)

  • Stand up AML monitoring — pattern rules (e.g., velocity, structuring) — even if our entity is upstream of the regulated piece, the aggregator will require this.
  • Data-protection compliance (CNPDCP) — privacy policy, data-handling docs.
  • If merchant count / volume crosses thresholds, revisit licensing track started in Phase 0.

Team allocation (ideally 4 by now)

Founder/PM: Growth, support, legal track
Backend eng: Reliability, fraud, Afrimoney via MOKO
Mobile eng: Localisation, funnel polish, merchant referral mechanic
+ Designer / 4th eng: UX iteration, second-platform polish

Exit signals (not strict, but useful)

  • Steady-state active merchants in the three-to-four-figure range, transacting regularly.
  • Cost per round-trip stable and modelled; effective MDR holds above the pawaPay round-trip cost.
  • You can take a weekend off without something breaking.

Phase 4   Direct-operator on-ramp parallel

6–18 months elapsed (mostly relationship work)

Goal: open commercial conversations with the operators directly — not to replace pawaPay yet, but to start the relationship clock so Phase 5 has something to switch onto. This phase is mostly NOT engineering time.

Workstreams

  • M-Pesa DRC commercial conversation via supportmpesa@m-pesa.cd — KYC pack, M-Pesa Services Agreement, pricing quote. Months of back-and-forth typical.
  • Airtel Money DRC — self-serve sandbox via developers.airtel.africa; per-country contract for production. Easier than M-Pesa.
  • Orange Money DRC — subscribe at an Orange DRC store as a KYA-compliant merchant; integrate Web Payment API for sandbox testing.
  • Engineering side: build sandbox prototypes against each direct API behind a feature flag; measure success rates vs pawaPay; cache learnings.

Output of this phase

  • Real per-leg rate quotes from at least M-Pesa and Airtel (the two largest, ~82% of DRC mobile money share between them).
  • Working sandbox integrations to each (behind a flag — not yet in production traffic).
  • Decision: is the direct path actually cheaper than pawaPay for our volume, and how much would the switch save?

Team allocation

Founder/PM: Most of the work — commercial outreach, contract review
Backend eng: Sandbox prototype each operator (~3–4 weeks each, behind flags)
Mobile eng: Unaffected — continues Phase 3 work
Why this is "elapsed" not "engineering" time: contract negotiations are slow and asymmetric. As a small player, expect months for M-Pesa, weeks-to-months for Airtel, weeks for Orange. Engineering work fits in the cracks.

Phase 5   Hybrid switch (own technical layer)

16–32 weeks engineering (only when Phase 4 contracts exist)

Goal: route the M-Pesa and Airtel legs through our own direct integrations (cheaper, faster); keep pawaPay as the fallback for Orange + Afrimoney + as a safety net for the operators we own.

Why hybrid not full

  • Direct contracts for M-Pesa + Airtel cover ~82% of DRC mobile money — most of the savings.
  • Orange and Afrimoney legs stay on pawaPay / MOKO until volume justifies separate contracts (or until they become uneconomic to route through aggregators).
  • Maintains fallback resilience — if direct operator API has an outage, we re-route via the aggregator for that leg automatically.

Engineering workstreams

  • Internal "switch" abstraction — define our own provider interface (collect, payout, status, refund); pawaPay, M-Pesa, Airtel, Orange (Web Payment), MOKO all implement it.
  • Routing logic — choose path per (operator, leg, retry) by configured policy; per-route success-rate telemetry.
  • Reconciliation — multi-source ledger reconciliation across providers; harder than the pawaPay-only world.
  • Compliance plumbing — direct contracts come with more direct AML/reporting obligations.
  • Float / treasury function — operator-side prefunded balances; sweep policy; FX position management.
  • Operations — recertification workflow as operator APIs change; runbooks.

Pre-requisites

  • Direct contracts with at least M-Pesa DRC and Airtel DRC (output of Phase 4).
  • Working capital available for operator-side prefund balances (typically >= 1 month of GMV).
  • Volume that materially justifies the build — see build-vs-rent analysis.
Don't start Phase 5 without Phase 4 deliverables. Building the switch without contracts means months of speculative engineering against APIs whose pricing you can't model.

Phase 6   Own the rails (BCC EMI licence) — optional

12–24+ months elapsed

Goal: become a BCC-licensed Electronic Money Institution. Full ownership of the rails, full regulatory weight. Only undertaken if scale justifies the $2.5M capital lock-up and the compliance ops cost.

Workstreams (mostly NOT engineering)

  • Capital: raise / commit US$ 2.5M paid-up minimum for the EMI subsidiary.
  • Legal / corporate: incorporate a DRC special-purpose EMI subsidiary; governance.
  • BCC application: business plan, risk framework, AML/CFT programme, IT controls, board, capital adequacy.
  • Compliance ops: FIU reporting pipeline (CENAREF/CENTIF); 10-year data retention; PEP/UN/OFAC screening.
  • Treasury: direct settlement to merchant bank accounts; FX desk; corporate banking.
  • Engineering: incremental — most of the technical work already done in Phase 5. The big new piece is regulator-grade audit logging.

Decision gates before starting

  • Monthly GMV at a level where Phase 5 hybrid savings make a Phase 6 commitment look like ~3–5 year ROI on the $2.5M tied up + ongoing comply-cost.
  • Strategic case: do we want to be a regulated EMI? Are there ancillary products (issuance, custody, lending) that only an EMI can do?
  • Capital availability without distorting the core business.
Most companies don't reach Phase 6. The build-vs-rent analysis in the main report shows that even at $1.5M monthly GMV, the math is roughly neutral — the case strengthens at higher volumes and with ancillary products. Plan as if Phase 5 is the destination unless / until the data argues otherwise.

Open decisions to make before Phase 1

  1. Mobile stack: React Native (shared codebase, fastest team velocity) vs Flutter (Google ecosystem, strong UI) vs native Swift+Kotlin (best performance / store integration, 2x effort). Default for a 2–4 person team: React Native.
  2. Backend stack: Python (FastAPI; strong libraries, francophone-Africa hireability) vs Node/TypeScript (mobile-friendly type sharing) vs Go (ops simplicity, performance). The backend has in fact been built in Python / FastAPI; the tech-stack note leaves the final call to the hired engineer.
  3. Hosting region: AWS Europe (Frankfurt) or GCP Europe — closest viable to DRC with mature compliance.
  4. Pass-through vs held-balance model: v1 pass-through (recommended) — instant settlement to the merchant, we never hold funds. Held-balance is an EMI-shaped decision.
  5. MDR (merchant fee) level — open pricing decision: the placeholder is 1%, but a pawaPay round-trip costs ~3.5–5%, so 1% is below cost. A viable MDR is likely ~5–7%+. The merchant absorbs this; the customer always pays the exact sticker price. Resolve before pricing the pilot.
  6. Beachhead rollout: sequence the ~40 lined-up gas stations + pop-up stores — all at once, or in clusters.
  7. Currency default: USD-first or CDF-first UX? (Both supported; default matters for cognitive load.)
  8. Brand language: French primary confirmed; Lingala / Swahili priority for Phase 3.
  9. Designer model: contract from Phase 0, or absorb into mobile engineer with a component library?
  10. Customer-support channel: WhatsApp Business + phone vs in-app chat. WhatsApp is cheaper and closer to DRC norms.
  11. Whether to start Phase 4 (operator-direct conversations) in parallel with Phase 1 or only after Phase 3. Earlier means the relationship clock runs sooner; later means we have data to bring to the table.

Resolving these in Phase 0

Each item above can be settled in 1–3 days of focused work in Phase 0. Spending the time now avoids weeks of rework later.
